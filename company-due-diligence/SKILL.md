# Company Due Diligence

Use this skill to run a disciplined, evidence-first company review. Work from primary sources whenever possible, distinguish fact from inference, and keep the output decision-oriented.

## Quick Start

1. Define the diligence objective, decision deadline, company type, and audience.
2. Set the scope: full diligence or a focused pass on market, product, financial, legal, or management risk.
3. Build a fact base from the best available sources. Prefer primary materials over commentary.
4. Analyze each relevant workstream, note open questions, and rank risks by severity and uncertainty.
5. Deliver a memo with the recommendation, supporting evidence, and next-step diligence requests.

If the user has not specified the purpose, assume the goal is to decide whether the company is attractive enough to merit deeper diligence.

## Workflow

### 1. Frame the Assignment

Establish these items before deep research:

- What decision is being supported: invest, acquire, partner, buy from, join, or benchmark.
- What stage the company is in: public, late-stage private, startup, bootstrapped, subsidiary, or distressed.
- What standard of proof is needed: quick screen, management meeting prep, IC memo, buyer diligence, vendor risk review.
- What matters most for the decision: growth quality, moat, product risk, unit economics, compliance, leadership, concentration, or execution.

Translate that into a short diligence plan. Name the workstreams you will cover and what "good" and "bad" would look like for each one.

### 2. Build the Fact Base

Gather the highest-quality available sources first. Use exact dates for time-sensitive claims.

- For public companies, start with filings, annual and quarterly reports, earnings materials, investor presentations, proxy statements, and transcripts.
- For private companies, start with the company site, product docs, pricing pages, hiring patterns, founder interviews, customer references, data-room materials, and reputable databases if available.
- For any company, add third-party reporting only after checking whether it is corroborated.

Read [references/source-priority.md](references/source-priority.md) before broad web research. Use it to decide which sources to trust and how to handle weak evidence.

Maintain a running evidence table with:

- Claim
- Evidence
- Source and date
- Confidence level
- Whether the point is fact, estimate, or inference

### 3. Review the Core Workstreams

Cover only the workstreams relevant to the decision, but explain any skipped areas. Use [references/diligence-checklist.md](references/diligence-checklist.md) to select the right checks.

Common workstreams:

- Business and market: problem, category, TAM realism, ICP, competition, differentiation, pricing power, market structure.
- Product and technology: product quality, roadmap credibility, technical debt, platform dependency, security, AI claims, reliability.
- Commercial traction: customer proof, retention hints, sales motion, pipeline quality, land-and-expand evidence, concentration.
- Financial quality: revenue composition, margin profile, burn, liquidity, working capital, unit economics, accounting quality.
- Legal and compliance: IP ownership, licenses, privacy, sector regulation, litigation, sanctions, export controls, employment issues.
- Management and governance: founder-market fit, turnover, incentives, board quality, control dynamics, capital allocation, disclosure quality.

For each workstream, answer:

- What is true?
- Why does it matter?
- What could go wrong?
- How confident are we?
- What should we ask for next?

### 4. Identify Red Flags and Diligence Gaps

Separate confirmed issues from unanswered questions.

- Confirmed issue: supported by strong evidence.
- Plausible concern: supported by partial or indirect evidence.
- Unknown: material topic with insufficient evidence.

Do not smooth over missing data. Missing information is itself a diligence finding when it blocks a decision.

Escalate especially hard on:

- Revenue or customer concentration
- Weak retention or engagement masking
- Aggressive non-GAAP storytelling without reconciliation
- Regulatory or licensing dependencies
- Key-person risk
- Heavy dependence on a platform, reseller, or single supplier
- Frequent strategy pivots without evidence of learning
- Claims of leadership or moat that do not survive competitor comparison

### 5. Synthesize the Decision

Turn the research into a recommendation, not just a summary.

State clearly:

- Overall view: attractive, mixed, unattractive, or insufficient evidence
- Top reasons to believe
- Top risks and how they could impair the thesis
- What would change your view
- What diligence should happen next

Use [references/memo-template.md](references/memo-template.md) as the default deliverable structure unless the user asks for a different format.

## Working Rules

- Prefer primary sources, then close-to-primary sources, then commentary.
- Attribute every material claim to a source and date.
- Distinguish observed fact from your inference.
- Quantify when possible, but do not invent precision.
- Compare claims against peers, not just against management framing.
- Be explicit about what you could not verify.
- For legal, regulatory, financial, or safety-critical claims, require stronger evidence and say when specialist review is still needed.
- If the user asks for a quick take, still surface the top uncertainties rather than pretending certainty.

## Deliverables

Default to one of these outputs unless the user specifies another:

- Diligence memo: best for an investment or acquisition decision.
- Red-flag summary: best for fast screening.
- Question list: best for management meetings or data-room follow-up.
- Comparison table: best for two or more companies.

Always end with a short "next diligence steps" section when there are unresolved material gaps.
