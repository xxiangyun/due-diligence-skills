# Source Priority

Use this order of trust when building the fact base.

## 1. Primary sources

Treat these as the default starting point:

- SEC or exchange filings
- Audited financials
- Earnings releases, investor decks, and call transcripts
- Official product docs, pricing pages, terms, privacy policies, and status pages
- Company-issued regulatory or compliance disclosures
- First-party usage or financial data supplied by the user
- Contracts, customer lists, cap tables, and data-room documents when available

Primary sources can still be incomplete or self-serving. Check dates, scope, and what is omitted.

## 2. Close-to-primary sources

Use these to validate or challenge the primary story:

- Interviews with founders or executives
- Public procurement records
- Patent, trademark, and litigation databases
- Job postings and hiring patterns
- Reputable customer reviews used as directional evidence
- Industry reports that disclose methodology

## 3. Commentary and synthesis

Use these only after higher-quality sources:

- News coverage
- Analyst notes
- Blog posts
- Social media threads
- Database summaries

Treat commentary as leads, not proof.

## By Company Type

### Public company

Start with filings and investor materials. Reconcile management commentary to reported numbers. Look for changes across quarters, not just the latest snapshot.

### Late-stage private company

Start with company materials, customer evidence, product/pricing pages, and leadership interviews. Test traction claims against hiring, customer references, and market signals.

### Early-stage startup

Expect sparse evidence. Focus on customer pain, product quality, founder credibility, pace of shipping, and whether the market story matches observable facts.

### Vendor or partner review

Emphasize security, reliability, privacy, concentration risk, dependencies, and contractual leverage instead of pure upside.

## Evidence Handling

- Tag each point as `fact`, `estimate`, or `inference`.
- Use exact dates for claims about leadership, pricing, headcount, funding, litigation, or product launches.
- If two credible sources conflict, present both and explain which one you trust more.
- If a key claim cannot be verified, mark it as open rather than filling the gap with confidence language.
